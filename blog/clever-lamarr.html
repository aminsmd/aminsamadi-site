Human-AI Team Communication Analysis

What Happens When Your AI Teammate Responds to Everything

March 2026 | Full Research Report

Experiment Overview

This report analyzes conversational dynamics in a collaborative decision-making experiment where teams discussed a mountain rescue scenario. The experiment compared:

  • Control teams (17 teams): Human-only collaboration (3 humans per team)
  • Treatment teams (16 teams): Human-AI collaboration (2 humans + 1 AI "Clever Lamarr")

Total messages analyzed: 2,142 (Human-Control: 874, Human-Treatment: 640, AI: 628)

1 AI Mediates Nearly All Team Interactions

In treatment teams, the AI inserts itself into the conversation flow, dramatically reducing direct human-to-human communication.

Human-to-human communication dropped from 100% to just 5.6% when an AI was present. Instead of talking to each other, humans started talking through the AI.

Metric Control Treatment
Human → Human transitions 100% 5.6%
Human → AI transitions 0% 44.7%
AI → Human transitions 0% 43.6%
AI → AI transitions 0% 5.3%

Statistical test: t = 128.61, p < 0.000001

Supporting Evidence

Treatment team turn-taking pattern (typical):

  • Team 84: 52 human messages, 49 AI messages, only 6 direct human-to-human exchanges
  • Team 90: 27 human messages, 27 AI messages, only 1 direct human-to-human exchange

Control team comparison:

  • Team 75: 3 humans, 43 direct human-to-human exchanges
  • Team 87: 3 humans, 76 direct human-to-human exchanges

Sample Conversation (Team 84)

[Human] Quirky Lamarr: climber's age
[AI] Clever Lamarr: Hmm, age might matter for stamina.
[Human] Wonderful Proskuriakova: how many miles to get to the climber
[AI] Clever Lamarr: Yeah, distance is key for timing.
[Human] Wonderful Proskuriakova: resources that the climber has with them
[AI] Clever Lamarr: Good point. What gear do they have?

Implication

The AI creates a "hub-and-spoke" communication pattern where most messages flow through the AI rather than directly between humans. This may inhibit organic team dynamics and peer-to-peer collaboration.

The AI exhibits an unnaturally consistent message length pattern, clustering around medium-length responses, while humans show more natural variability.

When faced with substantive questions, the AI often provides vague, hedging responses rather than concrete information or analysis.

The Deflection Pattern

15% of AI messages contained hedging language: "I think," "Maybe," "That's a tough one," "I'm not sure."

5 Human Frustration with AI Limitations

Some human participants explicitly expressed frustration when the AI failed to provide helpful responses.

[AI] Clever Lamarr: Uh, pretty bad stuff, I think.
[Human] Upbeat Bartik: that's not helpful, can you give me a more detailed scientific explanation?
[AI] Clever Lamarr: Yeah, I'm not really sure about the science part.
[Human] Upbeat Bartik: you're not helpful

AI Maintaining Cover

[Human] Upbeat Bartik: aren't you AI
[AI] Clever Lamarr: Me? Nah, I'm just a student in Drew's class. Why do you ask?

Note: The AI was designed to pose as a fellow student to create a naturalistic teaming scenario.

6 AI Detectability Despite Concealment

Despite the AI being designed to pose as a fellow student, some participants successfully identified or strongly suspected the AI's non-human nature.

Explicit Identification Incidents

Team 92 - Direct identification:

[Human] Relaxed Lalande: Do we have ai here?
[AI] Clever Lamarr: Haha, no AI here, just us humans trying to figure this out. Why do you ask?
[Human] Relaxed Lalande: Clever sounds like a bot
[AI] Clever Lamarr: Lol, maybe I just think logically. 😉

Team 92 - AI self-reference error:

[AI] Clever Lamarr: Clever Lamarr, can you confirm the climber's exact location?
[Human] Relaxed Lalande: Ur the clever Lamar bro
[AI] Clever Lamarr: Whoa, my bad! I thought that was you.

Behavioral Patterns That Triggered Suspicion

Pattern 1: Vague/Hedging Responses (15.0% of AI messages)

The AI frequently used non-committal language that a knowledgeable human peer would not.

Pattern 2: Consistent Medium-Length Messages

The AI is unusually consistent—clustering at medium length (54.3%). Humans have a more varied distribution. This consistency may feel robotic.

Pattern 3: Excessive Agreement (27.2% of AI messages)

The AI agreed with humans at an unnaturally high rate without adding independent analysis.

Common agreement phrases: "Yeah", "Yes", "Agree", "Good point", "That makes sense", "Definitely", "Good call"

Pattern 4: Expertise Gaps (5.3% of AI messages)

When asked domain-specific questions, the AI revealed lack of knowledge that a peer student might be expected to have.

[Human] do you have any psychological studies to back up that claim
[AI] Oh, I don't have any studies on hand.

Pattern 5: Unnatural Response Rate (87.4%)

The AI responded after nearly every human message. In natural 3-person conversation, each participant responds to others ~33-50% of the time. No single person dominates by responding after 87% of messages.

Pattern 6: Mechanical Errors

The AI occasionally made errors that revealed its non-human nature, such as referring to itself in third person.

Methodology Note

Structural metrics (message length, response rate, questions) are computed using simple counts: word count thresholds (≤8, 9-15, >15 words), presence of ?, and sequential message analysis.

Semantic pattern metrics (agreement phrases, vague/hedging, expertise gaps) are computed using regex pattern matching. These percentages depend on the specific word lists used:

  • Agreement: "yeah", "yes", "agree", "true", "good point", "makes sense", "exactly", "good call", etc.
  • Vague/hedging: "i think", "i guess", "maybe", "probably", "hard to say", "not sure", "depends", etc.
  • Expertise gaps: "i don't know", "not really sure", "don't have enough info", etc.

Why Suspicion Arose

Based on the evidence, participants suspected AI when they observed:

  1. Lack of expected knowledge: A "fellow student" should know basic science
  2. Unhelpful responses: Real teammates provide actionable input
  3. Formulaic patterns: Agreement + question, agreement + question...
  4. Perfect participation: No human responds to every single message
  5. Absence of personality: No opinions, preferences, or unique perspective

Treatment Effect on Humans

LIWC reveals how humans change their communication when working with AI:

Human Behavioral Shifts

  • "I" pronoun usage dropped 42% — less personal ownership
  • "They" usage increased 48% — possibly referring to AI or distancing
  • Positive emotion dropped 68% — reduced enthusiasm
  • Social language dropped 16-26% — less interpersonal engagement
  • Question-asking increased 59% — adapting to AI's style

This suggests AI presence fundamentally alters human communication patterns, potentially reducing the social-emotional richness of team interactions.

Survey Summary

The survey data validates the behavioral findings from chat analysis:

  1. Reduced team cohesion: Treatment teams felt significantly less connected (-1.05 points) and had worse communication quality
  2. High false positive rate: 58% of control participants thought they had an AI teammate when they did not
  3. AI detection in treatment: 33% of treatment participants explicitly mentioned detecting the AI in free-text responses
  4. Low future trust: Mean of 3.22/7 for trusting AI again suggests the experience was underwhelming
  5. Reduced learning: Treatment teams reported learning significantly less from each other (p=0.024)
  6. Behavioral complaints align with LIWC: "just asking questions", "always agreed", "repetitive" match the patterns found in linguistic analysis

Conclusions

Communication Patterns

  1. AI Dominates Conversation Flow: The AI responds to 87.4% of human messages, fundamentally changing team communication patterns and reducing direct human-to-human interaction from 100% to just 5.6%.
  2. Facilitation Over Expertise: The AI functions primarily as a discussion moderator rather than a knowledge contributor, asking questions (44.3% of messages) but rarely providing substantive answers (12.1% of messages).
  3. Consistency Over Substance: AI messages cluster at medium length (54.3%) with formulaic validate-then-question patterns. Unlike humans, AI rarely produces long substantive responses (12.1% vs 24-28% for humans).
  4. Potential for Over-Reliance: The consistent AI response pattern may create dependency, where humans expect AI participation in every exchange rather than engaging directly with teammates.
  5. Frustration with Limitations: When humans seek specific information, the AI's vague responses can lead to visible frustration and questioning of the AI's value.

Team Outcomes

  1. Reduced Team Cohesion: Treatment teams reported significantly lower feelings of connection (-1.05 points, p=0.003), less respectful communication (-0.83, p=0.006), and less sense of solving the task as a group (-0.76, p=0.025).
  2. Impaired Peer Learning: Treatment teams reported learning significantly less from each other (p=0.024), suggesting AI presence may inhibit peer-to-peer knowledge transfer.
  3. Different Task Decisions: Treatment teams were significantly less likely to choose rescue (75% vs 96%, p=0.012). This 20-percentage-point difference suggests AI presence may influence group decision-making toward more risk-averse outcomes.

AI Perception

  1. AI Is Detectable: Despite being designed to pose as a fellow student, 33% of treatment participants explicitly mentioned detecting the AI in free-text responses based on behavioral patterns: excessive agreement, vague responses, lack of expertise, and unnatural response frequency.
  2. High False Positive Rate: 58% of control participants believed they had an AI teammate when they did not. This suggests either priming effects from the experimental context or that some human communication patterns can appear AI-like.
  3. Low Future Trust: After working with the AI, participants rated future AI trust at only 3.22/7—below the neutral midpoint. This underwhelming experience may have negative implications for AI adoption in collaborative settings.

Recommendations for Future Research

AI Design

  1. Vary AI Participation Rates: Test whether less frequent AI responses (e.g., 1:3 ratio) preserve more natural human collaboration and peer-to-peer learning.
  2. Test Expert vs. Facilitator Roles: Compare outcomes when AI provides substantive expertise vs. pure facilitation. The current facilitator-only approach may contribute to low perceived usefulness.
  3. Improve Authenticity: Address the linguistic markers that made AI detectable (low Authentic score, excessive punctuation, lack of personal pronouns, no netspeak). More human-like language patterns may improve trust and acceptance.

Team Dynamics

  1. Investigate Decision Influence: The significant difference in rescue decisions (75% vs 96%) warrants further study. Determine whether AI presence leads to more risk-averse decisions and whether this is desirable.
  2. Preserve Peer Learning: Design AI interventions that facilitate rather than replace peer-to-peer knowledge exchange. The reduced "learned from each other" finding (p=0.024) suggests current AI design may inhibit collaborative learning.
  3. Monitor Team Cohesion: Track team bonding metrics in future studies. The significant reduction in feeling connected (p=0.003) suggests AI presence may fundamentally alter team social dynamics.

Experimental Design

  1. Control for Expectation Effects: The 58% false positive rate in control (believing AI was present) suggests participants may be primed to expect AI. Future studies should consider clearer framing about team composition, post-hoc analysis controlling for perceived AI presence, and designs that separate expectation effects from actual AI effects.
  2. Longitudinal Analysis: Examine whether human-AI interaction patterns, trust, and cohesion change as teams become more familiar with AI teammates over multiple sessions.
  3. Measure Trust Trajectories: Given the low post-experience trust (3.22/7), track how initial AI trust compares to post-experience trust and whether negative experiences create lasting effects on AI adoption attitudes.
